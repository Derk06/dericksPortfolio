Machine Learning

In late 2022, I started a udmey course on TensorFlow & Machine Learning which was around 66 hours. In this course, I built TensorFlow models using computer vision, convolutional neural networks, and natural language processing. I learned how to integrate machine learning into tools and applications and developed various machine learning models using the latest TensorFlow 2. By building image and text recognition algorithms with deep neural networks and convolutional neural networks, I worked with real-world images to visualize how a computer "sees" information and plotted loss and accuracy to track model performance. I also applied deep learning techniques to time series forecasting. I have documented my learning on google collab, you can click the button below.


Then in 2024, I began researching reinforcement learning for my upcoming school project, I was able to find a book about reinforcement learning and for the next 3 months I read through the entire book and learned the fundamentals of reinforcement learning (RL), starting with the key elements such as agents, environments, states, actions, and rewards. I explored the basic concepts of RL and learned how it differs from other machine learning paradigms. The book introduced me to Markov Decision Processes (MDP), including the Markov property, Markov chains, the Markov Reward Process, and the Markov Decision Process, which are foundational to understanding RL. I delved into the mathematical essentials of RL, including expectations, action space, policy types (deterministic and stochastic), and concepts like episodes, horizons, and discount factors. I learned how to analyze the value function, Q function, and the differences between model-based and model-free learning. I also explored various types of environments, such as deterministic vs. stochastic, discrete vs. continuous, and single vs. multi-agent environments, as well as their applications in different scenarios. The book provided a hands-on experience with the Gym toolkit, where I set up my machine, installed Anaconda and the Gym toolkit, and created my first Gym environment. I explored various environments, including classic control and Atari game environments, and learned how to generate episodes, select actions, and analyze state and action spaces. I even worked with more complex environments like Box2D, MuJoCo, and robotics, and implemented algorithms in these settings. I studied the Bellman equation in depth, including the value function, Q function, and the Bellman optimality equation.


I applied dynamic programming techniques, such as value iteration and policy iteration, to solve problems like the Frozen Lake problem. The book also covered Monte Carlo methods, including prediction and control tasks, first-visit and every-visit Monte Carlo prediction, and the implementation of these methods in environments like the blackjack game in the Gym library. Understanding temporal difference (TD) learning was another key part of the book. I learned about TD prediction and control algorithms, such as SARSA and Q-learning, and applied them to environments like the Frozen Lake Gym Environment. I compared the DP, MC, and TD methods to understand their strengths and weaknesses. The book also featured a case study on the multi-armed bandit (MAB) problem, where I explored various exploration strategies like epsilon-greedy, softmax exploration, upper confidence bound, and Thompson sampling. I applied these strategies to real-world problems, such as finding the best advertisement banner using bandits. In the deep learning foundations section, I explored the relationship between biological and artificial neurons, the structure of artificial neural networks (ANNs), and various activation functions. I learned about forward propagation in ANNs, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), and convolutional neural networks (CNNs). I also delved into advanced topics like generative adversarial networks (GANs), understanding their architecture, and learning the loss functions.The book introduced me to TensorFlow, where I learned about computational graphs, sessions, variables, constants, placeholders, and TensorBoard for visualization. I built a handwritten digit classification model using TensorFlow and explored TensorFlow 2.0 with Keras for model definition, compilation, training, and evaluation. I further studied deep Q networks (DQN) and their variants, including double DQN, DQN with prioritized experience replay, and dueling DQN. I learned about replay buffers, loss functions, and target networks, and applied these concepts to playing Atari games using DQN. I also explored advanced techniques like deep recurrent Q networks.


The book covered policy gradient methods, including their intuition, derivation, and variance reduction methods. I implemented algorithms like policy gradient with reward-to-go and REINFORCE with baseline, applying them to tasks like cart-pole balancing. In the actor-critic methods section, I learned about advantage actor-critic (A2C) and asynchronous advantage actor-critic (A3C), exploring their architecture and applying them to environments like mountain car climbing. I also studied deep deterministic policy gradient (DDPG), twin delayed DDPG (TD3, the algorithim used for my project), and soft actor-critic (SAC), understanding their components, networks, and algorithms, and applying them to tasks like swinging up a pendulum. The book concluded with advanced topics like TRPO, PPO, and ACKTR methods, where I studied trust region policy optimization, proximal policy optimization, and actor-critic using Kronecker-factored trust region. I also explored distributional reinforcement learning, including categorical DQN and quantile regression DQN, and applied these methods to Atari games. Finally, I studied imitation learning and inverse reinforcement learning, including supervised imitation learning, DAgger, deep Q learning from demonstrations, and maximum entropy inverse reinforcement learning. I explored generative adversarial imitation learning (GAIL) and its formulation. The book also introduced me to the Stable Baselines framework for deep reinforcement learning, where I created agents, evaluated, stored, and loaded trained models, and integrated custom environments. The book wrapped up with cutting-edge topics in reinforcement learning, such as meta reinforcement learning, model-agnostic meta learning (MAML), hierarchical reinforcement learning, and imagination-augmented agents, providing me with a deep understanding of the frontiers of reinforcement learning.
